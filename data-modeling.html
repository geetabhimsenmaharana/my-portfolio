Data Modeling

Data Modeling

What It Means (Simple Explanation)

Data modeling is the process of organizing business information in a way that makes it easy to store, understand, and answer questions from the data.
Think of it like designing a library. Before placing books on shelves, you decide how they should be organized so people can quickly find what they need. Data modeling does the same thing for data.


What It Means (Technical Explanation)

Data modeling is the process of defining how data entities, relationships, attributes, and business events are structured within a database or data warehouse to support efficient storage, querying, reporting, and analytics.

Simple Mental Model

Fact Table = What happened?

Dimension Table = Who, What, Where, When?

Grain = What does one row represent?

Star Schema = How everything connects together for reporting.

Data Modeling = Designing all of the above so the business can answer questions from data efficiently.



REAL PROJECT EXAMPLE

Pest Control Service Data Warehouse



Good data modeling starts with understanding the business process, and also writing SQL.

I was asked to design a data model that would improve reporting and analytics for business stakeholders. Below is the approach I followed using the STAR framework.

S — Situation

At Pest Control India (PCI), I worked as a SQL Analyst supporting reporting and analytics initiatives. Business stakeholders needed better visibility into service performance, customer behavior, revenue trends, and operational metrics. However, the raw operational data was spread across multiple tables and was not optimized for reporting or analysis.

T — Task

My responsibility was to design a scalable data model that would allow analysts, business users, and reporting tools to efficiently answer business questions such as:

  • What is the total revenue generated?
  • Which services are generating the most revenue?
  • Who are the highest-value customers?
  • How do revenue and service trends change over time?
  • Which regions or branches perform the best?

A — Action

I began by understanding the business process before designing any tables.
First, I identified the primary business event. In this case, the key event was a customer receiving a pest control service. immediately creating tables.

Next, I defined the grain of the fact table.

One row represents one product purchased within an order.

Defining the grain early ensures consistency and prevents future reporting issues.


After defining the grain, I identified the fact table and dimensions.


Finally, I would organize the warehouse using a Star Schema where the fact table sits at the center and dimension tables provide business context.

R — Result

The resulting model would provide a clean, scalable structure for analytics and reporting. Business users could easily analyze revenue, product performance, customer behavior, and sales trends without querying complex operational systems.

The model would also support future growth by allowing additional dimensions and metrics to be incorporated while maintaining reporting consistency and query performance.

Fact Table

What It Means (Simple Explanation)

A fact table stores the activities or events that happen in a business.

Think of it as a record of "what happened."

Examples:

  • A customer placed an order
  • A technician completed a service
  • A payment was received
  • A product was sold

If something happened and can be measured, it usually belongs in a fact table.


What It Means (Technical Explanation)

A fact table stores measurable business events and typically contains numeric metrics along with foreign keys that connect to dimension tables.

Examples of measures include:

  • Revenue
  • Quantity
  • Cost
  • Profit

Examples of keys include:

  • Customer_ID
  • Product_ID
  • Date_ID


Dimension Table

What It Means (Simple Explanation)

A dimension table provides details about the people, products, locations, or things involved in an event.

Think of it as information that helps explain the fact.


If the fact answers:

"What happened?"

The dimension answers:

  • Who?
  • What?
  • Where?
  • When?

What It Means (Technical Explanation)

A dimension table stores descriptive attributes that provide business context for facts and are joined to fact tables using keys.

Customer Dimension:

  • Customer Name
  • Customer Type
  • City

Product Dimension:

  • Product Name
  • Category
  • Brand

Technician Dimension:

  • Technician Name
  • Region


Grain

What It Means (Simple Explanation)

Grain defines exactly what one row of data represents.


Before building any table, you must answer:

"What does a single row mean?"

For example:

  • One row = One customer order
  • One row = One service visit
  • One row = One product sold

Grain is the foundation of a good data model.


What It Means (Technical Explanation)

Grain is the lowest level of detail represented by a record in a fact table and defines the uniqueness and business meaning of each row.

A clearly defined grain prevents duplicate records, incorrect aggregations, and inaccurate reporting.



Star Schema

What It Means (Simple Explanation)

A star schema is a way of organizing data where one central table stores business events and several surrounding tables store descriptive information.

It looks like a star.

  • The center contains what happened.
  • The surrounding tables explain the details.

This structure makes reporting and analysis easier.

What It Means (Technical Explanation)

A star schema is a dimensional modeling design pattern consisting of a central fact table connected directly to multiple dimension tables through foreign key relationships.

The model is optimized for analytical workloads, reporting, aggregation, and business intelligence tools such as Power BI, Tableau, and Looker.

Key Learnings

  • Identify business processes before designing tables
  • Define the grain first
  • Separate facts from dimensions
  • Design for analytics, not transactions
  • Build scalable star schemas for reporting
